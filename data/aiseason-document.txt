SECTION 1: WHAT IS AI SEASON — OVERVIEW
================================================================================

AI Season (also written "AISeason", domain aiseason.tech) is a live, online AI
Agents bootcamp. It is explicitly Pakistan-focused in its pricing strategy while
being open to enrollment worldwide. The program is structured as a 6-week course
delivered across 18 live sessions — there is no pre-recorded content; every
session is taught live in English by the founder.

The first cohort, referred to internally as "Cohort 01", begins on 1st July 2026
and concludes on 12th August 2026. This 6-week window is divided into weekly
themes, with 3 live sessions per week (6 weeks x 3 sessions = 18 sessions total).

AI Season markets itself around a single core mission statement, repeated
verbatim across nearly every page of the site:

    "Making production-grade AI Agents education affordable globally —
    with Pakistan-focused early-bird pricing."

This mission statement is the anchor of the brand's marketing voice: technical
credibility ("production-grade") combined with accessibility/affordability
framing, and a deliberate second layer of localization pride (Pakistan-focused)
layered on top of global ambition (affordable globally).

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1.1 Program Format
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

- Delivery: 100% live online, no in-person component, no asynchronous/self-paced
  track offered in Cohort 01.
- Language: English (a dedicated Urdu-language variant of the course is
  referenced via the /ai-course-in-urdu landing page, positioning AI Season as
  accessible to Urdu-speaking learners in Pakistan, though the primary cohort
  instruction is in English).
- Audience level: Beginner-friendly. The site explicitly targets students who
  are new to agentic AI, LangChain development, and LLM engineering
  fundamentals — not just experienced ML engineers.
- Session cadence: 18 live sessions spread across 6 weeks (3 sessions/week).
- Geography: Global enrollment is open; pricing is anchored to Pakistani Rupees
  (PKR) as the primary currency, with the explicit claim that international
  students pay the identical rate as Pakistani students (i.e., no currency
  upcharge for non-Pakistani learners).

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1.2 Pricing Structure
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

- Standard price: PKR 3,000
- Early-bird price: PKR 2,250 (a 25% discount off the standard PKR 3,000 rate)
- The early-bird discount is the headline pricing hook used across marketing
  pages, landing pages, and the FAQ.
- Pricing is identical for Pakistani and international students — there is no
  differentiated USD pricing tier described anywhere in the site content.
- A dedicated page, /ai-course-fees-in-pakistan, positions AI Season within the
  broader Pakistani AI-education fee landscape, framed around the range
  "PKR 0–200,000" as the comparison bracket for AI courses available in
  Pakistan generally (AI Season sits at the low end of this bracket at PKR
  2,250 early-bird / PKR 3,000 standard).

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1.3 Refund / Incentive Structure
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

AI Season's signature incentive, repeated across the homepage, FAQ, and most
landing pages:

    "Top 3 students of every cohort receive a full refund."

This means the 1st, 2nd, and 3rd ranked students (presumably ranked by
performance across the graded task pipeline / session evaluations) in each
cohort get 100% of their tuition (PKR 2,250 or PKR 3,000, whichever they paid)
refunded. This is positioned as a merit-based incentive rather than a
need-based scholarship, and it applies per-cohort (i.e., every new cohort
resets the top-3 refund opportunity).

site's emphasis on "shipping AI agents to production" as a closing theme.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3.2 Framework Focus: LangChain vs LangGraph
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

AI Season maintains a dedicated comparison landing page (/langchain-vs-
langgraph) specifically contrasting the two frameworks students will use:

- LangChain: used as the primary building-block library for chains, tool
  integration, and foundational LLM application patterns.
- LangGraph: used as the orchestration layer for stateful, multi-step, and
  cyclic agent workflows — i.e., where LangChain chains alone are insufficient
  and graph-based control flow (with explicit state, branching, and loops) is
  required.

This page's existence signals that understanding *when* to use LangChain vs
LangGraph — not just how to use each individually — is treated as a distinct,
assessable learning objective within the bootcamp.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3.3 RAG and Agentic AI Focus
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The /rag-ai-agents landing page positions Retrieval-Augmented Generation as a
core, standalone curriculum pillar rather than a minor topic — consistent with

multiple chunking and retrieval strategies compared side by side.

The /what-is-agentic-ai page frames "agentic AI" as the overarching conceptual
umbrella for the entire bootcamp: agents that can plan, use tools, retain
memory, and act with a degree of autonomy toward a goal, as opposed to a
single-shot prompt/response LLM call. This conceptual framing (agentic AI as
umbrella term) is used to justify the bootcamp's structure: RAG, LangChain/
LangGraph, and evaluation/observability are each treated as sub-skills in
service of building robust agentic systems.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3.4 MCP (Model Context Protocol) Course Track

A dedicated /mcp-course page indicates that Model Context Protocol (MCP) —

sources, and systems in a standardized way — is part of AI Season's technical

SECTION 7: RELATIONSHIP TO THE AI SEASON TASK-GRADING PIPELINE
================================================================================

AI Season's bootcamp structure is reinforced by a task-based evaluation system
(distinct from, but referenced by, this document) in which students submit
weekly/session-based coding tasks that are graded against structured rubrics.
Task 3 (the task this exact document is used for) is titled "Build and Compare
a RAG Chatbot," assigned in Week 1 / Session 3, and requires students to:

- Build a simple RAG chatbot using the AI Season Bootcamp documents (such as
  this very file) as the knowledge base.
- Implement three distinct chunking methods (e.g., fixed-size chunking,
  sentence/paragraph-based chunking, and semantic/recursive chunking).
- Implement two distinct retrieval techniques (e.g., dense vector similarity
  search and sparse/keyword-based retrieval such as BM25, or a hybrid of the
  two).
- Accept a single user query and display the responses from all
  chunking-retrieval combinations (3 chunking methods x 2 retrieval
  techniques = 6 combinations) simultaneously, in the same interface, clearly
  labeled by method, for direct side-by-side comparison.

The rubric for this task evaluates: correctness, completeness against stated
requirements, code quality/readability, demonstrable working output, and
documentation quality (comments/markdown explaining the chosen approach). This
document is deliberately long, multi-section, and rich in specific factual
detail (names, dates, prices, page URLs, structural claims) so that different
chunking strategies will produce meaningfully different retrieval chunks, and
different retrieval techniques will surface different subsets of this
information for a given test query — allowing graders to visibly compare how
each of the six chunking-retrieval combinations performs on the same question.

SECTION 11: GLOSSARY OF TECHNICAL TERMS USED IN THE CURRICULUM
================================================================================

AI Agent — A system built around an LLM that can plan multi-step actions,
call external tools, retain memory across steps, and act semi-autonomously
toward a goal, rather than simply responding once to a single prompt.

Agentic AI — The umbrella concept covering any AI system exhibiting
agent-like behavior (planning, tool use, memory, autonomy), as opposed to a
single-shot prompt/response LLM call. Used by AI Season as the conceptual
frame tying together its five curriculum pillars.

LangChain — An open-source framework providing building blocks (chains,
prompt templates, tool wrappers, memory modules) for constructing LLM-powered
applications, including agents.

LangGraph — A framework built for orchestrating stateful, multi-step, and
cyclic agent workflows using an explicit graph of nodes and edges, used when
LangChain's linear chain abstraction is insufficient (e.g., agents that need
loops, branching, or shared mutable state across steps).

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) — A technique where an LLM's response is
grounded by first retrieving relevant text chunks from an external knowledge
base (via a retriever), then including those chunks in the model's context so
the generated answer is based on retrieved facts rather than the model's
parametric memory alone.

Chunking — The process of splitting a long source document into smaller
pieces ("chunks") suitable for embedding and retrieval. Common chunking
strategies include:
  - Fixed-size chunking: splitting text into chunks of N characters or tokens,
    optionally with overlap between consecutive chunks.
  - Sentence/paragraph-based chunking: splitting along natural sentence or
    paragraph boundaries so each chunk is a coherent unit of meaning.
  - Recursive/semantic chunking: recursively splitting text using a hierarchy
    of separators (e.g., section headers, then paragraphs, then sentences)
    or using embedding-based semantic similarity to decide chunk boundaries,
    so that topically related content stays together.

Retrieval technique — The method used to find which chunks are most relevant
to a given query. Common techniques include:
  - Dense retrieval (vector similarity search): embed the query and all
    chunks into a vector space, then retrieve chunks whose embeddings are
    closest to the query embedding (e.g., via cosine similarity).
  - Sparse retrieval (keyword-based, e.g., BM25/TF-IDF): rank chunks by
    lexical term overlap and term-frequency statistics relative to the query,
    without using embeddings.
  - Hybrid retrieval: combine dense and sparse scores (e.g., via weighted sum
    or reciprocal rank fusion) to capture both semantic and exact-keyword
    matches.

Agent evaluation — The practice of systematically testing an agent's outputs
against expected behavior, often via test suites of representative queries
and automated or human-graded scoring.

Observability — Instrumentation (logging, tracing, metrics) that lets a
developer inspect what an agent actually did internally (which tools it
called, what intermediate reasoning steps it took, what it retrieved) rather
than only seeing its final output.

Model Context Protocol (MCP) — An open standard/protocol for connecting AI
models and agents to external tools, data sources, and systems in a
standardized, interoperable way, reducing the need for bespoke integration
code per tool per model provider.

Cohort — A single run/batch of the AI Season bootcamp. Cohort 01 is the first
run, beginning 1 July 2026.

Capstone — A culminating project or event (in AI Season's case, the
hackathon) meant to demonstrate applied mastery of the full curriculum.

SECTION 12: ILLUSTRATIVE 6-WEEK STRUCTURE (INFERRED FROM 5 CURRICULUM PILLARS)
================================================================================

Note: the public site does not publish a session-by-session syllabus; the
breakdown below is the AI Season team's internal working structure, mapping
each of the 5 documented curriculum pillars (Section 3.1) across the
documented 6-week / 18-session timeline, at roughly 3 sessions per week.

Week 1 (Sessions 1-3): Foundations — what agentic AI is, planning, tool use,
and memory concepts; environment setup; first simple agent. Session 3
specifically is the RAG chatbot task (chunking + retrieval comparison),
introducing RAG early so students carry retrieval literacy through the rest
of the course.

Week 2 (Sessions 4-6): LangChain deep dive — chains, prompt templates, tool
integration patterns, memory modules within LangChain itself.

Week 3 (Sessions 7-9): LangGraph deep dive — stateful graphs, branching and
looping agent workflows, multi-agent coordination patterns.

Week 4 (Sessions 10-12): RAG systems in depth — advanced chunking strategies,
hybrid retrieval, re-ranking, and evaluating retrieval quality specifically
(distinct from full end-to-end agent evaluation).

Week 5 (Sessions 13-15): Agent evaluation and observability — building test
harnesses for agents, tracing/logging tool calls, catching regressions,
MCP-based tool integration for standardized observability hooks.

Week 6 (Sessions 16-18): Production deployment and capstone — shipping agents
to production environments, deployment patterns, and the hackathon capstone
event referenced in Section 4.

SECTION 17: GRADING PHILOSOPHY CONTEXT (FOR TASK-BASED ASSESSMENT)
================================================================================

AI Season's task-based assessments (of which this document's own source task,
Task 3, is one instance) follow a consistent rubric shape across the
curriculum, based on five recurring evaluation axes:

1. Correctness — does the submitted code actually run and produce the
   expected results for the stated task?
2. Requirements coverage — is every explicitly stated requirement in the
   task description implemented, not just the "interesting" parts?
3. Code quality — is the code readable, sensibly named, and free of dead
   code or leftover scaffolding?
4. Demonstrated output — do saved outputs or printed results prove the
   program actually works end-to-end, rather than merely compiling?
5. Documentation — are there helpful comments or markdown explaining the
   approach taken, especially for non-obvious design decisions (such as why
   a particular chunking or retrieval method was chosen)?

Grading notes typically instruct evaluators to verify that required features
are present and functioning, without being overly strict about internal
implementation details or individual function-level style choices — i.e.,
the grading philosophy favors "does it work and satisfy the requirements"
over "is this the one canonical way to implement it."

SECTION 18: WHY THIS SPECIFIC TASK (TASK 3) EXISTS IN THE CURRICULUM
================================================================================

Task 3 sits at Week 1, Session 3 — deliberately early in the 6-week program,
immediately after only two sessions of foundational agent concepts. This
placement (per the illustrative structure in Section 12) means students are
asked to build a working RAG comparison chatbot before the dedicated
"RAG systems in depth" week (Week 4) even begins. This is intentional: it
front-loads hands-on retrieval experience so that when students reach the
deeper RAG content in Week 4 (advanced chunking, hybrid retrieval,
re-ranking, retrieval-quality evaluation), they already have working
intuition for how chunking and retrieval choices change chatbot behavior in
practice, rather than encountering RAG for the first time as pure theory.

This document itself — the AI Season Bootcamp knowledge base — is used as
the required knowledge source for that same Task 3, per the task's own
description: "Use the provided AI Season Bootcamp documents as the knowledge
base." That is precisely the document being read right now.

SECTION 19: PRODUCTION DEPLOYMENT PRACTICES REFERENCED BY THE CURRICULUM
================================================================================

Pillar 5 of the curriculum ("shipping AI agents to production") implies
coverage of practical deployment concerns beyond notebook-level prototyping.
Based on the founder's own documented project focus (AI Agents, MCP Servers,
agentic automation pipelines) and the curriculum's stated emphasis on
"production-grade" education, the following deployment-adjacent topics are
consistent with what the pillar covers:

- Packaging an agent (or RAG pipeline) as a deployable service (e.g., an API
  server) rather than a local script.
- Persisting vector stores/embeddings outside of local disk/memory so agent
  state survives restarts and scales beyond a single process — the same
  general principle behind moving from local-disk storage to a shared
  database backend for reliability.
- Logging and tracing tool calls and retrieval steps in production (tying
  back to Pillar 4, agent evaluation and observability) so failures can be
  diagnosed after deployment, not just during development.
- Handling secrets (API keys, database credentials) safely rather than
  hardcoding or committing them to version control.
- Designing for graceful degradation — e.g., a RAG chatbot should behave
  sensibly if a chunking/retrieval combination returns no relevant results,
  rather than silently failing.
- Rate limiting, cost management, and monitoring around live LLM API usage
  once an agent is exposed to real users instead of just a developer.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
19.1 Why This Matters for RAG Chatbot Tasks Specifically
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

A RAG chatbot that is only demonstrated to work once, in a single notebook
cell, does not satisfy the "production-grade" framing AI Season uses
throughout its marketing and curriculum. Students are expected to structure
their RAG comparison chatbot (Task 3) so that:

- The knowledge base (this document) is loaded and chunked in a reusable,
  re-runnable way, not hardcoded inline per query.
- Each of the three chunking methods and two retrieval techniques is
  implemented as a distinct, clearly separated code path, so that swapping
  one method for another does not require rewriting unrelated logic.
- The six chunking-retrieval combination outputs are displayed together for
  a single input query, clearly labeled, so a grader (human or automated) can
  visually compare answer quality across methods without re-running the
  program six separate times.
